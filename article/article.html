Mayonnaise

A brief history


Dressing to impress

Mayonnaise is a fantastic addition to any sandwich or salad, invented in 1974 by Alfred D. Mayonnaise, this dressing has been on the shelves and in the bellies of many people all around the world. Made from an emulsification of eggs, oil and either vinegar or lemon juice, it has a thick creamy textrue and a sour, tangy flavor.

A rich history

Before mayonnaise, those seeking a tangy yet creamy addtiton to their sandwiches or salads would have to call upon the local tang man. The tang man was reponsible for keeping the sour. Different regions often had different recipes and flavors to their sour. For example, in Mexico, the sour was made with the mumblings of women above the age of 60 whereas in Ethiopia, sour was made from the pain of factory workers injured due to negligence.

Since the invention of mayonnaise however, the demand for sour has dropped off rapidly. This has led to much displeasure from tang man unions

The cream of the crop

Mayonnaise is categorised into 5 different grades of quality. Yellow, mellow, off-white, white and perfect. Grading is based upon criteria such as flavor, texture, color and tang.

Other uses of mayonnaise

Mayonnaise has a multitude of uses beyond culinary. These uses include, musical, industrial as a personal lubricant

Personal safety

You do need to take care with mayonnaise, and it's important to know how the product that is in your sandwich or on your salad was prepared. If it was made with raw eggs, then you need to check that they are British Lion stamped. "Freshly made Bearnaise, Hollandaise and tartare sauces can also contain raw egg, so be careful with these too,” warns nutritionist Dr Rana Conway. “You're most likely to come across them in a farm shop or a fancy restaurant, particularly if you are abroad. So it is always worth asking if mayonnaise or other sauces are made with unpasteurised egg." When you are buying mayonnaise to eat during pregnancy, choose the varieties that are in a jar in the non-refrigerated aisle of the supermarket, becase they would have been made with pasteurised (heat-treated) eggs. These include brands like Hellmann’s and Heinz and stores own brands. Be careful with mayonnaise and sauces that have been homemade, or that are sold as fresh from delis, restaurants or food markets. If you are in the least bit unsure then it's safest to avoid.
